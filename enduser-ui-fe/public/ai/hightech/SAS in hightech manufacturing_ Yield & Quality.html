Enhancing Yield Management and Defect Reduction

A SAS Technical Deep Dive for Taiwan's High-Tech Manufacturers

The core challenge in semiconductor and PCB manufacturing—managing immense complexity and data volumes to maximize yield and minimize defects—is precisely what SAS aims to address. SAS's multi-faceted approach, combining robust data handling, advanced statistical methods, and sophisticated AI/ML techniques, is well-documented and designed to deliver tangible operational outcomes. This document provides a technical validation of these capabilities with enhanced detail and illustrative diagrams.

1. SAS Methodologies for Yield Prediction and Optimization: Technical Validation and Operational Outcomes

SAS's "Quality Mining" and "Low Yield Analysis" are structured methodologies built upon the SAS Viya platform. They leverage SAS Viya's powerful data processing (CAS - Cloud Analytic Services) and analytical engines (like VDMML) to deliver tangible results.

1.1 Data Extraction and Preparation (The Foundation)

Challenge Validation: High-tech manufacturing environments generate massive, often siloed datasets from diverse systems like MES (Manufacturing Execution Systems), EWS (Early Warning Systems), FDC (Fault Detection and Classification), CP (Characterization Program), WAT (Wafer Acceptance Test), process history, and equipment logs. This data is frequently "dirty" (containing errors, outliers, inconsistencies), "summarized" (lacking necessary granularity), or "sampled" (not representing the full process), demanding significant engineering hours for preparation to make it suitable for reliable analytics.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (SAS Data Integration Studio / SAS Studio Flows / SAS Viya Data Preparation):

These tools automate the creation and management of robust big data pipelines at scale. They facilitate ETL (Extraction, Transformation, Loading) and ELT processes, connecting to and processing data from varied sources including relational databases (Oracle, PostgreSQL), data warehouses, Hadoop, flat files, and streaming data endpoints. Key capabilities include:

  • Automated Data Ingestion: Scheduled or event-driven data pulls from source systems.
  • Data Cleansing & Validation: Identifying and handling missing values, outliers, and inconsistencies using predefined rules or data quality functions.
  • Data Transformation: Aggregating, joining, transposing, and deriving new variables to structure data appropriately for analysis (e.g., creating time-based features, aligning sensor data with lot/wafer context).
  • Scalable Processing: Leveraging SAS Viya's CAS engine for distributed, in-memory processing of large datasets.

Operational Outcome: Significant reduction in manual data preparation time (often by >50%), consistent data quality across projects, and the ability to efficiently handle terabytes of manufacturing data. This frees up valuable engineering resources to focus on analysis and interpretation rather than data wrangling.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (SAS Enterprise Guide - EG):

EG is frequently used as a user-friendly, project-based front-end for data access, manipulation, ad-hoc analysis, and reporting. It's particularly valued for its ease of use in accessing SAS Viya's CAS for high-performance, multi-threaded data processing. Engineers can create reusable process flows to automate routine data preparation tasks. The TSMC case specifically highlights leveraging EG with Viya CAS to boost data preparation performance and enable efficient EDA.

Operational Outcome: Empowers engineers and analysts (including those who are not expert programmers) to perform complex data manipulations and ad-hoc queries more efficiently, accelerating the initial stages of any yield analysis project and fostering a data-driven culture.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Data "Cooking" and Analytical Data Marts):

SAS enables the transformation of raw, often disparate, manufacturing data into "analytics-ready data" by creating structured Analytical Data Marts or Analytical Base Tables (ABTs). This involves:

  • Systematic handling of missing values (e.g., imputation based on statistical measures or predictive models).
  • Deriving new and meaningful features (feature engineering), such as calculating summary statistics over time windows, creating interaction terms, or transforming variables for better model performance.
  • Ensuring high data quality and consistency suitable for advanced modeling.
  • The "Automated Feature Machine" within SAS Viya (VDMML) can automate many data transformation and feature creation tasks, incorporating best practices to generate optimal features for models, thereby reducing manual trial-and-error.

Operational Outcome: Higher quality input data for models, leading to more accurate and reliable predictions. Automation in feature engineering saves considerable time and effort, allowing for more sophisticated features to be developed and tested, ultimately improving model performance and insight generation.

1.2 Yield Prediction Modeling (The Core Analytics)

Challenge Validation: Predicting End-of-Line (EOL) yield at various critical process steps using vast and complex upstream process data (from hundreds or thousands of parameters) is a significant hurdle. The specific factors and their interactions impacting yield are often unknown or not clearly understood, making traditional statistical modeling insufficient.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (SAS Visual Data Mining and Machine Learning - VDMML):

VDMML, a key component of SAS Viya, provides a comprehensive, interactive, and often automated environment for building, evaluating, and deploying predictive models. It supports a wide range of supervised learning algorithms crucial for yield prediction:

  • Decision Trees, Gradient Boosting, Random Forests: These ensemble methods are extensively used to classify outcomes (e.g., good/bad wafer, assigning to high/low yield bins based on EOL test results) and, importantly, to identify the most influential manufacturing stages or process parameters affecting yield through variable importance measures (e.g., Gini importance, permutation importance). They excel at handling mixed data types and capturing non-linear relationships.
  • Regression Models (Linear, Quantile, Generalized Linear Models, Partial Least Squares - PLS): These are employed to predict continuous yield values directly or specific quality inspection metrics based on a set of input parameters. PLS is particularly useful when dealing with many, potentially collinear, predictor variables (common in FDC data).
  • Neural Networks & Deep Learning: For capturing highly complex, non-linear relationships and interactions between numerous process parameters and yield outcomes, which are common in intricate semiconductor or advanced PCB manufacturing processes.

Operational Outcome: More precise EOL yield predictions at critical junctures, enabling proactive adjustments. Clear identification of key process variables, equipment, and stages that significantly impact yield, allowing engineers to focus their improvement efforts. This was a key achievement in the TSMC engagement, leading to better process understanding and control.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (High-Performance Computing - CAS):

SAS Viya’s in-memory Cloud Analytic Services (CAS) architecture is designed for massively parallel, distributed processing of large datasets. This is particularly vital in semiconductor manufacturing where data volumes from FDC, WAT, and MES can easily reach terabytes or petabytes. CAS enables significantly faster model training, scoring, and iterative model refinement by distributing computations across multiple nodes and cores.

Operational Outcome: Drastic reduction in model development and deployment times (from days/weeks to hours/days), allowing for more frequent model updates to adapt to process changes and quicker responses to emerging yield issues. Enables analysis of full datasets rather than relying on samples.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Automated Machine Learning - AutoML):

SAS Viya includes AutoML capabilities (e.g., within Model Studio) which automate significant parts of the model development lifecycle. This includes automated feature engineering, algorithm selection (testing multiple algorithms like logistic regression, gradient boosting, neural networks, etc.), and hyperparameter tuning to find the best performing model for yield prediction or classification tasks with minimal manual intervention.

Operational Outcome: Faster deployment of effective yield prediction models. Democratization of model building, enabling process engineers (Citizen Data Scientists) to develop and deploy robust models without deep data science expertise, thus addressing talent shortages.

1.3 Optimization and Root Cause Identification (The Actionable Insights)

Challenge Validation: Root cause analysis (RCA) for yield excursions is often a lengthy, manual, and inconclusive process, relying heavily on tribal knowledge. Identifying optimal conditions for process recipes to maximize yield can also be unclear and involve costly trial-and-error.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (AI-Assisted Yield Management):

SAS uses the insights derived from the trained AI models to perform faster and more accurate RCA. This includes leveraging model interpretability features:

  • Variable Importance Rankings: From tree-based models (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) to identify the most influential parameters.
  • Partial Dependence Plots (PDP) & Individual Conditional Expectation (ICE) plots: To understand how changes in specific parameters affect predicted yield.
  • LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) or SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values (if supported/integrated): To explain individual predictions and identify contributing factors for specific low-yield events.

Operational Outcome: Quicker and more accurate identification of the true root causes of yield loss, enabling more effective and targeted corrective actions, moving beyond simple correlations to understand causal drivers.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Low Yield Analysis Workflow):

This is a structured, often automated, approach within SAS to systematically diagnose yield problems:

  • Problem Definition & Data Selection: Clearly defining the yield issue (e.g., specific defect type, affected product/process step) and automatically extracting relevant data (process parameters, equipment IDs, timestamps, material IDs, upstream/downstream data) for the affected lots/wafers versus a "golden" or high-yield population.
  • Automated Pattern Discovery (Classification/Clustering): Grouping defective wafers based on spatial defect patterns on the wafer map (e.g., edge defects, center clusters, scratches using spatial clustering algorithms like DBScan) or by parametric signatures (e.g., wafers failing specific electrical tests, using K-Means or hierarchical clustering on WAT data).
  • Difference Testing & Commonality/Variability Analysis: Employing statistical tests (e.g., T-tests, ANOVA, Chi-squared) and decision tree analysis to automatically compare distributions of hundreds/thousands of process parameters, equipment settings, and material characteristics between high-yield and low-yield groups (or different defect clusters). This helps pinpoint statistically significant differences and identify critical process steps, specific equipment (EQP), chambers (CHB), or material batches contributing to yield variations.
  • Interactive Data Mart Creation & EDA: Generating temporary, focused data marts that consolidate relevant data for the specific yield issue. Engineers can then use SAS Visual Analytics or SAS Enterprise Guide for interactive Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) – creating box plots, scatter plots, correlation matrices, parallel coordinate plots – to visually explore relationships highlighted by the automated analysis and validate findings.

Operational Outcome: A systematic and significantly accelerated approach to RCA, reducing analysis time for complex yield issues from days/weeks to hours (e.g., from 82 hours to 10 hours in reported cases). Provides engineers with statistically validated, targeted areas for investigation and corrective action.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Process Simulation / Digital Twins for Yield):

SAS supports the creation and use of AI-driven digital twins to simulate manufacturing processes specifically for yield optimization. The predictive yield models (linking process parameters to yield outcomes) form the core of this digital twin. This allows engineers to:

  • Virtually test the impact of new recipe parameters or process adjustments on predicted yield *before* implementation on the factory floor.
  • Identify optimal operating windows and robust recipe settings that maximize yield while minimizing variability.
  • The photomask blanks film formation case, where ML predicts quality values (a proxy for yield) and optimization algorithms search for optimal control parameters, is a prime example of this approach.

Operational Outcome: Reduced time and cost for process optimization and yield ramp-up for new products/processes. Identification of more robust process windows leading to sustained high yield. Minimized risk associated with implementing process changes.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Early Warning Systems - EWS for Yield):

SAS solutions, particularly leveraging SAS Event Stream Processing (ESP), enable the development of EWS that predict and flag process anomalies or deviations likely to lead to yield excursions *in real-time*. Models trained on historical data to identify precursors to low yield events are deployed in ESP to score live sensor and process data. This was a key benefit for the HDD manufacturer, which reduced its early warning time for potential yield excursions from 78 days to 5 days or less.

Operational Outcome: Proactive prevention of yield loss by allowing engineers to intervene *before* out-of-specification conditions occur or low-yield material is produced. Leads to more stable and higher average yield rates (e.g., improvement from 60% to 80% in some projects).

2. AI-Driven Defect Detection, Classification, and Root Cause Analysis: Technical Validation and Operational Outcomes

SAS leverages AI/ML not only for overall yield prediction but also for the specific challenges of defect management, including automated detection, accurate classification, and efficient root cause analysis.

2.1 Automated Defect Detection & Classification

Challenge Validation: Manual defect detection is notoriously slow, error-prone, and inconsistent, especially for subtle or complex defects. Traditional SPC methods may not effectively catch complex, emerging defect patterns or subtle process deviations leading to specific defect types.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Machine Learning Algorithms - VDMML):

Algorithms such as decision trees, gradient boosting, random forests, and support vector machines in SAS VDMML are used to build models that can automatically classify products or components (e.g., good/bad wafer, presence/absence of specific defect types like scratches, particles, voids) based on parametric, sensor, or image data. These models also help identify the manufacturing stages or process parameters most influential in causing these specific defects.

Operational Outcome: Increased accuracy, speed, and consistency in defect classification compared to manual methods. Identification of critical control points for specific defect reduction, enabling targeted process improvements.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (PCB Specifics - Defect Mapping & Image Pattern Analysis):

For PCB manufacturing, SAS provides specialized solutions that support detailed defect mapping by linking the physical X-Y coordinates of defects (often from Automated Optical Inspection - AOI, Automated Electrical Inspection - AEI, or Automated Visual Inspection - AVI systems) with specific process parameters (e.g., variations in temperature, pressure, speed during lamination, etching, or plating) and material information. This enables automated failure cause analysis based on defect coordinates and clustering techniques. This involves:

  • Data Integration: Combining defect data (images, coordinates, types) from AOI/AEI/AVI systems with contextual data from MES (lot history, routing), SPC (process measurements), FDC (equipment sensor data), and material tracking systems.
  • Spatial Analysis & Clustering: Using clustering algorithms (e.g., K-Means, DBScan, or spatial statistics) on defect coordinate data to identify defect hotspots (areas with unusually high defect density), recurring spatial patterns on the PCB surface (e.g., defects aligned in a specific direction, repeating patterns across multiple boards), and correlating these patterns with specific equipment (e.g., a particular printer, etcher, or plating tank), process steps, or even specific vendor materials.
  • Parameter Correlation & Defect Signatures: Linking defect locations, types, and frequencies with critical process parameters (e.g., copper thickness, line width/spacing, via characteristics, solder mask registration) and FDC sensor readings from the tools that processed those specific areas of the PCB. This helps build "defect signatures" associated with certain process deviations or equipment states.

Operational Outcome: Faster identification of systematic defect sources in PCB manufacturing (e.g., a misaligned print head, a faulty plating bath, an issue with a specific batch of laminate). Enables targeted corrective actions on specific machines or process steps. Improved understanding of how subtle process variations translate to physical defects, leading to more robust process design and control.

2.2 AI-Driven Root Cause Analysis (RCA) for Defects

Challenge Validation: Traditional RCA methods for specific defect types can be time-consuming, often relying on tribal knowledge and manual data sifting across multiple systems, leading to inconclusive results or addressing symptoms rather than true root causes.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Model Interpretability):

SAS models (e.g., decision trees, logistic regression, gradient boosting, explainable AI techniques like LIME/SHAP if integrated/available) provide clear insights into variable importance, partial dependence plots, and individual prediction explanations. These features help engineers pinpoint which factors (specific process parameters, equipment settings, material batches, environmental conditions, operator actions) are most strongly correlated with, or causally contributing to, the occurrence of particular defects.

Operational Outcome: More focused, data-driven, and faster RCA, reducing the time spent on investigating irrelevant factors. Increased confidence in RCA findings due to statistical validation.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Automated Workflow for RCA):

As part of the Low Yield Analysis framework (which can be adapted for specific defect RCA), SAS Viya can automate many steps in the RCA process. This includes automatically identifying statistically significant differences in numerous process parameters between defective and non-defective populations (or populations with different defect types), highlighting potential root causes through statistical tests (t-tests, ANOVA, chi-square) and decision tree/rule induction analysis.

Operational Outcome: Standardization and significant acceleration of the RCA process for defects. Enables engineers to quickly narrow down potential causes from thousands of variables, leading to quicker problem resolution and prevention of recurrence.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Integration of Diverse Data for Holistic RCA):

By creating a unified data repository (as demonstrated in the Murata case, where quality, cost, and delivery data were integrated for holistic analysis), SAS enables a more comprehensive RCA. This allows engineers to consider data from all relevant manufacturing stages (e.g., incoming material quality certificates, specific tool processing history from FDC, environmental sensor data, operator logs, upstream/downstream test results) when investigating the origins of specific defects.

Operational Outcome: More comprehensive and accurate RCA by uncovering complex interactions between different parts of the manufacturing process or supply chain that might contribute to defects. Avoids overlooking crucial factors that reside in siloed data systems.

3. Leveraging SAS Computer Vision for Quality Inspection: Technical Validation and Operational Outcomes

SAS Computer Vision offers an automated, AI-powered approach to visual inspection, directly addressing the limitations of manual methods by providing higher accuracy, speed, and consistency.

Challenge Validation: Manual visual inspection is inherently prone to human error due to fatigue and subjectivity, leading to inconsistency in defect detection. It's often a bottleneck in high-volume production and may not be feasible for 100% inspection of all units, especially where defects are microscopic or require sustained attention to detail.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Offline Model Training - SAS Visual Data Mining and Machine Learning):

The process begins with developing robust deep learning models:

  • Data Ingestion & Preparation: Raw video or image data from production line cameras, microscopes, or AOI systems is loaded into SAS Viya's CAS for distributed processing. This includes collecting a diverse set of labeled images representing both "good" products and various types of "defective" products.
  • Image Processing/Augmentation: Images undergo pre-processing (e.g., resizing to standard dimensions, color conversion, normalization, noise reduction using filters like Bilateral Filter) and extensive augmentation (e.g., rotation, flipping, scaling, brightness/contrast adjustments, adding synthetic noise). Augmentation artificially expands the training dataset, improving model robustness and its ability to generalize to unseen variations in lighting, orientation, or defect appearance. Techniques like Canny Edge detection can be applied if relevant for highlighting defect features.
  • Deep Learning Model Generation: Deep learning models, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) tailored for image classification (e.g., ResNet, VGG) or object detection architectures like YOLO (You Only Look Once) and its variants (e.g., Tiny YOLO for edge deployment), are trained in SAS VDMML or SAS Model Studio. These models learn to identify and classify defects based on the labeled image datasets. The output is a deployable model, often in SAS's ASTORE (Analytic Store) file format, which encapsulates the trained model logic, weights, and parameters.

Operational Outcome (Offline Training): Creation of highly accurate and specialized visual defect detection/classification models tailored to the specific products, defect types, and imaging conditions of the manufacturer. These models can often surpass human accuracy for specific, well-defined visual inspection tasks.

SAS Technical Solution & Operational Outcome (Online Scoring - SAS Event Stream Processing - ESP on the Edge):

The trained models are then deployed for real-time inspection:

  • Model Ingestion & Edge Deployment: The trained ASTORE model is ingested by SAS ESP, which is designed for low-latency, high-throughput stream processing. ESP projects containing the vision models can be deployed at the edge, on servers or industrial PCs located close to the production line cameras, minimizing network latency.
  • Real-Time Image/Video Processing: Live video footage or streaming images from production line cameras are captured and processed by ESP in real-time. This includes necessary pre-processing like frame extraction from video and resizing (e.g., to 416x416 for Tiny YOLO input).
  • In-Stream Scoring & Tracking: Each image or frame is scored in-stream using the deployed deep learning model to detect defects, identify objects of interest (e.g., components on a PCB, specific regions on a wafer), and track them over time. This involves:
    • Object Detection (e.g., Tiny YOLO): Identifying objects of interest within the frame and drawing bounding boxes around them, along with classifying them (e.g., "good component," "misaligned component," "solder defect").
    • Keypoint Detection: For more granular analysis, specific keypoints within the detected objects (e.g., corners of a chip, leads of a component) can be identified after cropping and resizing the bounding box region. This is useful for precise alignment checks or dimensional measurements.
    • Kalman Filtering: This is a crucial technique used for robust object tracking in video streams. It associates real-time temporal information (object movement and state across frames) with the (often time-independent) deep learning model outputs, helping to maintain object identity, predict future positions, and smooth out noisy detections, even with temporary occlusions.
    • Data Association for Tracking: Techniques like Intersection over Union (IoU) between bounding boxes in consecutive frames are used to associate detected objects, ensuring that the same physical object is tracked consistently as it moves through the camera's field of view.
  • Alert Triggering & PLC/MES Integration: Based on the scoring results (e.g., a defect is detected above a certain confidence threshold, an object is misplaced, a critical dimension is out of tolerance), SAS ESP can trigger immediate alerts to operators (e.g., via dashboards, visual signals) or higher-level systems. Crucially, ESP can integrate directly with Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs) or Manufacturing Execution Systems (MES) to initiate automated responses on the production line, such as stopping a conveyor belt, diverting a defective product to a rejection bin, adjusting a machine parameter, or flagging a lot for further inspection.

Operational Outcome (Online Scoring): Real-time, 100% automated visual inspection for targeted defect types. Significant reduction in inspection errors and escapes compared to manual methods. Immediate feedback and automated action for quality deviations, preventing further processing of defective units and minimizing scrap. Increased throughput due to faster inspection cycles. Consistent inspection quality 24/7.

Operational Outcome: Ability to analyze comprehensive datasets for deeper insights, rather than relying on sampled or summarized data, leading to more accurate models and root cause identification.
